1 Abstract
In this task, we conducted a detailed data analysis, tried diﬀerent feature engineering techniques
and implemented various methods to do the prediction based on the banking dataset. Our aim
is to ﬁnd out the model that could achieve the best F1 score with hyper tuning. Models with
relatively good performance are listed below. The best F1 score we achieved is 0.6436 by Stacking
and 0.6467 by LightGBM (GOSS).

4 Model Construction
In this section, we discuss our experimentation results with Random Forest, Neural Network,
XGBoost, Gradient Boosting Decision Tree (GBDT), LightGBM and Stacking.
4.1 Neural Network
We used Tensorﬂow and Keras for the implementation of artiﬁcial neural network. Since the
objective is obtaining a probability value, we chose sigmoid as the last layer. Tahn and relu
can both capture non-linear relationships between diﬀerent features. A too deep neural network
tends to cause overﬁtting problem. Therefore, we only consider the models of a scaling layer,
one forward propagation layer and a sigmoid layer.
From the tuning process, batch size is tuned to 10, which generally outperform 5, 20 and 25 for
all the models we tried. We set epoch to 100 since 500 epochs do not always bring improvement.
Root Mean Square Prop (rmsprop) performs around 1% better than Adaptive Moment Estima-
tion (adam) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (sgd). With 6 nodes in the forward propagation
layer, F1 exceeds 0.6 (for both relu and tanh activation function). We adjusted the number of
nodes and 6 performs better.
We have the following observations:
1. Tahn as an activation function performs well in terms of the model accuracy while it is
generally slower than relu since relu is faster during the process of gradient descent.
2. A too small middle layer (2 or 3 nodes) cannot perform well, meaning that information
is densed into too few nodes. A middle layer with 6 nodes or above generally has a moderate
performance with F1 around 0.6.
3. Tensorﬂow initially assigns random weights to each node. A good weight assignment
contributes to the performance improvement.
The best F1 we achieved is 0.617, which is obtained from the model in ﬁgure 17 with rmsprop
method, batch size of 10. We did not go much further in Artiﬁcial Neural Networks for the
following reasons: 1) There are a lot of diﬀerent combinations of parameters. 2) Since each
training contains at least 50 to 100 epochs, it takes around 2 minutes to ﬁnish training. It
is relatively slower than other simpler methods. 3) Neural Network is more suitable for large
dataset, while this dataset has only 7500 records. Therefore, the improvement might be limited.

4.6 Stacking
We use sklearn to apply Stacking. Based on the results of our previous part, we choose lightGBM
(with GOSS), XGBoost and GDBT as our level 1 model. Since when adding 3 new features we
got the best result so far with lightGBM, we also add these 3 features into Stacking and tune
XGBoost with 3 new features according.
After tuning, we ﬁnd that with (objective=’binary:logistic’, n estimators=57, learning rate=0.2,
max depth=4, min child weight=9, eta=0, gamma=3.5, subsample=0.9, colsample bytree=1,
random state=319), we could get relatively good XGBoost resutlt around 0.63 with 3 new fea-
tures added in. For the second level, we use sklearn to apply MLR, which is a good model here
by experience.
The stacking model we build here is modiﬁed a bit. We build the level 1 models based on the
whole given training data set, but only use a subsample of data to do the level 1 prediction and
drop others (draw from training dataset, by train test split) with random state to improve the
result. That is, the data put into level 2, MLR, is also a subsample of the prediction results of
the level 1 models and the corresponding real result (Exited ).
By trying around 300 random state with diﬀerent ranges and tune the sample size of dropping
form 0.1 to 1, we found our stable best result 0.6436233611442193 with size 0.900000000001 and
random state 372. We will discuss the possible explanation of the relatively good result of the
model build like this in Discussion 2.7.2.
It is true that the result we get here is not that high as in LightGBM (with GOSS boosting),
but we also noticed that Stacking is far more stable than LightGBM.
5 Conclusion
In task 2, our best F1-measure is obtained by LightGBM with GOSS Boosting. Stacking with
XGBoost, Gradient Boosting Decision Tree and LightGBM(GOSS) as level 1 models and linear
regression as level 2 also works well and is more stable than LightGBM itself. GDBT, XGBoost,
Lightgbm (GBDT) and Random Forest also perform well and relatively stable (if set subsample,
colsample bytree equals 1 and max features to be the maximum, or tuning them together with
random state). Neural Network does not work that well (but also reasonably good), which might
be because of the relatively small sample size and limited number of features. We may summarize

6 Discussion
For task 2, we would like to discuss the following issues, concerns and possible future works.
6.1 Regarding the Dataset, Evaluation Metric and Feature Engineer-
ing
In section 2.1.3 we notice that the training data set with label Exited = 0 is far more than that
with Exited = 1, the dataset is imbalanced and we are interested on the minority class (Exited
= 1), so the accuracy can be misleading and the metric we used here, F1-score, might draw a
good balance between precision and recall. However, F1-score might not be the best metric for
model selection because if we have two models one with high precision but low recall, the other
with high recall while low precision, their F1-score might be quite similar. We propose that for
future work, we may consider other metrics, for instance, ROC curve. And because this problem
is similar to a fraud detection problem, we may consider the threshold as the highest recall at a
relatively good range of precision.
Also, regarding the dataset only, we try to balance the training dataset regarding Exited = 1
and 0 by only treat a subsample of training data with Exited = 0 and reduce the proportion
of (Exited = 0)/(Exited = 1), however, there was no signiﬁcant improvement. We think this is
caused by the relatively small sample size.
In section 2.1.4, we found that regarding the parameter Balance, we detected many zeros while
there are many customers with non-zero and relatively high balance. So we tried to add one new
feature, IsBalancePositive, into the dataframe. However, as shown in ﬁgure 12, it seems that it
is not strongly related to Exited. And, it is a bit surprising that, actually, the customers with
positive balance are more likely to exit. However, by adding in the feature into Random Forest
the result is not that good and even drop and when adding into XGBoost the result is also not
improved. So ﬁnally we just eliminate this feature.
6.2 Regarding the Models
LightGBM:
As pointed out in section 2.3.5.2, diﬀerent encoding methods (get dummies and label encoding)
may lead to diﬀerent results. For instance, for LightGBM with GOSS Boosting, get dummies
11
works better than label encoding, but in LightGBM wit GDBT Boosting, label encoding actu-
ally wins. The diﬀerence between these two methods is, when using label encoding the result
is ordinal, that is, the distance between, say for Geography, Spain and Germany and France is
ﬁxed. But when it comes to get dummies, the result is binary and there’s no ”equal distance”
assumption. This may lead to diﬀerent results.
Stacking:
In section 2.3.6, we used stacking and build the model in an relatively unusual way that when
putting in data into the linear regression level, we only put in a subset of the predictions of level
1 models and the corresponding real Exited data. However, it seems that we cannot surly explain
why we can get better result by doing so because of the ’Black box’ nature of Stacking. We tried
to see the speciﬁc data of the sample but the reason is not that obvious. For instance, the ratio
of Exited of the sample we use, around 20.7% is quite similar to that of the whole training set
(20.8%). We guess the reason why using such a small level 2 training set brings better result is
that it reduce the level of over-ﬁtting.
For the future work, we may try to:
1) Adding in more level 1 models with relatively good results. By experience, this may lead to
better results. Also, the stacking model we build here may also have a lot of space to improve;
2) Because by trial and error we ﬁnd that our strategy of sampling the data into level 2 works,
we consider trying other measures, for instance, sampling before the model building (we actually
tried doing sampling before building but the results were not that good, we consider trying more
cases with more model and see whether it can be improved);
3) We did not include validation here and we may include validation step in order to overcome
over-ﬁtting issues.
6.3 Thoughts on Random State
In Task 2, we use the combination of random
state and other parameters to ﬁx the relatively
good results. However, it might not be a good way in reality because:
1. We notice that the same random state may generate diﬀerent pseudo random number on
diﬀerent systems (for example, the random states good for Windows and Google Colab may not
work well on MacOS). By random.setstate() we can ﬁx this issue to some extent.
2. In reality, the ”good” models relied on some limited ”random” states may not be reliable, as
the random state may work for this dataset only. What we aim at is to predict the results us-
ing new data. Thus, we cannot take chance and value the high scores only, stability is also crucial.
